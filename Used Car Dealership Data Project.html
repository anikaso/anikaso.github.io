Question 5¶

Depending on the model of a car and the year of manufacture, used car prices can vary. As of August 2004, the manager of the car dealership where the ToyotaCorolla.csv data set was collected has Toyota cars with different years of manufacture(in particular, cars manufactured in the categories of the years 1998 - 2004). To meet different customer needs for used cars, the manager of the Toyota car dealership offering the promotion claims that he maintains the following inventory proportions of Toyota cars by Year of manufacture(MFG_Year): 1998(22%), 1999(28%), 2000(11%), 2001(8%), 2002(10%), 2003(9%), and 2004(12%).

The goal of this problem is to assess whether the actual observed proportions of toyota cars in the ToyotaCorolla.csv data set, grouped by Year of manufacture(MFG_Year), supports the manager's claim or represents a significant departure from the claim.

Using the following hypotheses:

Null Hypothesis: The proportions of used Toyota cars by year of manufacture shows that the data is a random sample drawn from a population having the proportions claimed by the manager.

Alternative Hypothesis: The proportions of used Toyota cars by year of manufacture does not show that the data is a random sample drawn from a population having the proportions claimed by the manager.

We would reject the null hypothesis at the 0.05 significance level because our p-value (of 0) is less than our alpha of 0.05. This is because the tail beyond the observed test statistic is 0 so the observed test statistic is far from what the null hypothesis predicts. The data supports the alternate hypothesis better. In simple terms, because the p-value is 0, it provides very strong evidence that the proportions of used Toyota cars by year of manufacture does not show that the data is a random sample drawn from a population having the proportions claimed by the manager. Since the calculated p-value is much less than 0.05, it suggests that it is highly unlikely to observe that the proportions of used Toyota cars by year of manufacture does infact show that the data is a random sample drawn from a population having the proportions claimed by the manager. Therefore, we conclude that the observed proportions of used Toyota cars by year of manufacture significantly differ from the manager's claimed proportions, indicating a lack of support for the manager's inventory claims.

def difference_of_means(table, num_var, categ_var):
    """Takes name of the table, column label of numerical variable, column label of group/categorical variable
    Returns the difference between sample means of the two groups"""
    reduced = table.select(categ_var, num_var)
    means_table = reduced.group(categ_var, np.average)
    means = means_table.column(1)
    observed_difference = means.item(1) - means.item(0)
    return observed_difference

def one_simulated_difference(table, num_var, categ_var):
    shuffled_labels = table.sample(with_replacement=False).column(categ_var)
    shuffled_table = table.select(num_var).with_column('Shuffled Label', shuffled_labels)
